The terminology describing interaction types reflects the amount of information provided by the individual experiments used to measure an interaction. An experiment may provide only very general information (e.g. that a set of proteins may be purified together) or may provide detailed information about specific physical contacts or covalent bonds. The table below defines the terms used within DIP; please note that these terms may convey different information than their common usage might imply.




Interaction Type Description PSI-MI Controlled
Vocabulary Term
association DIP 'associations' are groups of proteins that form one or more physical complexes. 'Association' is used when experimental evidence is too weak to guarantee that all reported interacting molecules are present within the same physical complex. For example, some affinity experiments report interactions between a matrix-bound bait molecule (green in the figure) and additional prey molecules. These experiments may not distinguish between prey molecules bound together in the same complex (i.e. yellow, red, and pink at the top of the figure) and prey molecules bound in separate complexes (i.e. red versus blue in the figure). There is not enough information to designate a 'physical' interaction, so 'association' is used instead. association (MI:0914)
physical 'Physical' interactions in DIP describe a physical complex formed by all of the listed molecules. In other words, each pair of interacting molecules has been observed within the same physical complex. An experiment reporting a 'physical' interaction does not eliminate the possiblity that additional unidentified proteins provide a bridge connecting proteins within the complex. physical association (MI:0915)
direct Molecules participating in a 'direct' interaction have been shown to directly contact one-another (i.e. they have van der Waals contacts). Note that a homo-oligomer indicates a direct interaction, even though some subunits may not be in direct contact. direct interaction (MI:0407)
covalent In a 'covalent' interaction, all participating molecules are connected by covalent bonds. For proteins, the most common sub-type of covalent interaction is the disulfide bond (MI:0408), formed by a sulfur-sulfur bond between two cysteins. covalent binding (MI:0195)
