Contents of DIP: nodes and edges

DIP keeps track of proteins and their interactions. Graphically, proteins can be represented by nodes (circles) and interactions by edges (lines):



 The above graph shows the protein-protein interaction network of the kinetochore, the protein complex responsible for separating chromosomes during eukaryotic cell division. Notice that some of the edges, the curved ones, indicate a self interaction: an interaction between a protein and another copy of itself.

DIP nodes (proteins)
Each protein node in DIP is assigned a unique identifier of the form 'DIP-###N' ('N' for node). Each node is also cross-referenced to at least one of the major protein databases UniProt and RefSeq. DIP additionally stores some basic information about each protein, including name, organism, and a description.

DIP edges (interactions)
Each interaction edge in DIP is assigned a unique identifier of the form 'DIP-###E' ('E' for edge). DIP stores information such as the region involved in the interaction, the dissociation constant and the experimental methods used to identify and characterize the interaction.

Classifying interactions

Interactions may be classified according to the level of contact between the interacting proteins. Thus, interactions may be:
  • direct, if the interacting proteins touch each other directly;
  • physical, if the proteins physically associate, with the possibility that another molecule mediates the association; or
  • functional, if the proteins are linked by a related function, regardless of whether they physically associate.
DIP currently records direct and physical interactions. For functional interactions, try the Prolinks database.
